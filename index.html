Emotion & Well-Being

Chapter 6
Emotion — Brief, specific response involving appraisals, experiences, expressions, and physiology; helps meet social goals. Differs from moods (which last hours/days) and disorders (weeks/months).
Appraisal Theory — Emotions arise from interpretations (appraisals) of events as pleasant/unpleasant, novel, fair, threatening, etc. How you construe a situation determines what emotion you feel.
Four Components of Emotion — (1) Appraisal/experience, (2) Expressive behavior, (3) Physiological changes, (4) Action tendencies/goal-directed behavior.
Universality of Facial Expressions (Ekman) — Paul Ekman studied the Fore people of Papua New Guinea (no Western media exposure). Both Fore and Americans accurately matched 6 basic emotions: happiness, surprise, sadness, anger, disgust, fear. Accuracy rates 68–98%. Key Study
6 Basic Emotions (Ekman) — Happiness, surprise, sadness, anger, disgust, fear. Recent work extends to 15+ including pride, awe, embarrassment, envy, etc.
Constructivist vs. Evolutionary Approach — Evolutionary: emotions are adaptive, biologically based, universal. Constructivist: culture shapes what emotions we feel and express; focal emotions vary by culture.
Focal Emotions — Emotions especially common in a particular culture. E.g., anger more focal in honor cultures; shame/embarrassment more focal in interdependent cultures (China has 113 shame words vs. ~25 in English).
Affect Valuation Theory (Tsai) — Cultures value emotions that promote their ideals. Americans value excitement; East Asians value calmness. Influences consumer behavior, media, and leadership.
Emotion Regulation — (1) Reappraisal: reinterpreting causes; (2) Acceptance: viewing emotions as fleeting; (3) Suppression: minimizing outward expression. Interdependent cultures suppress more positive expressions.
Social Functional Theory — Emotions coordinate social interactions; enable forming, maintaining, negotiating relationships. Emotions signal commitment and build trust.
Oxytocin — Chemical that fosters commitment in relationships; produced in hypothalamus. Promotes trust and generosity within groups, but can enhance bias against outgroups (de Dreu).
Broaden-and-Build Hypothesis (Fredrickson) — Positive emotions broaden thoughts/actions; build social resources. Negative emotions narrow attention to specific details.
Happiness Components — (1) Life satisfaction (evaluative); (2) Emotional well-being (moment-to-moment ratio of positive to negative emotions). Also: eudaimonic happiness (meaning/purpose).
Affective Forecasting — Predicting future emotions. People are often poor forecasters due to immune neglect and focalism.
Immune Neglect — Failing to account for the psychological immune system when predicting unhappiness. We underestimate our resilience (e.g., "luckies vs. leftovers" breakup study).
Focalism — Focusing too much on the focal event while ignoring other life factors that also affect happiness. "Impact bias."
Duration Neglect — The length of an experience has surprisingly little effect on remembered pleasure; peak and endpoint matter most (Fredrickson & Kahneman).
Pursuit of Happiness (Evidence-Based) — Social connections, gratitude, giving to others, experiences > material goods, awe. Money helps up to ~$75K/year (Kahneman & Deaton) but recent data (Killingsworth) suggests happiness increases with income.
Jamieson et al. (2016) — Stress Reappraisal Empirical Article — Biopsychosocial model: challenge (resources ≥ demands) vs. threat (demands > resources). Reappraising arousal as helpful improves exam performance. DVs: (1) exam performance, (2) cardiovascular reactivity, (3) evaluation anxiety. Reappraisal group showed better outcomes than placebo control.
Cultural Differences in Desired Emotions — East Asians prefer calm, low-arousal states; Americans prefer excited, high-arousal states. This predicts everything from children's books to political rhetoric.

Attitudes, Behavior & Rationalization

Chapter 7
Attitude — An evaluation of an object along a positive-negative dimension. Three components: (1) Affective (feeling), (2) Cognitive (belief), (3) Behavioral (action tendency).
LaPiere Study — Toured US with a Chinese couple; only 1/250 establishments refused service. But ~90% said they wouldn't serve Chinese people when later surveyed by mail. Showed attitudes often don't predict behavior. Key Study
Why Attitudes Fail to Predict Behavior — (1) Conflict with other determinants (norms, habits); (2) Introspection misleads when attitude basis is emotional; (3) Mismatch between general attitude and specific target.
Implicit Attitudes & IAT — Implicit Association Test measures automatic evaluations people may be unaware of or unwilling to report. Reveals associations between attitude objects and positive/negative stimuli.
Cognitive Dissonance Theory (Festinger) — Inconsistency between thoughts/actions creates aversive arousal (dissonance) → motivation to restore consistency. Typically by changing attitude to match behavior.
Post-Decision Dissonance — After choosing between two equally attractive options, we bolster the chosen alternative and derogate the unchosen. Racetrack study: bettors more confident AFTER placing bet. Key Study
Effort Justification — Reducing dissonance by convincing yourself that something difficult or unpleasant was worth it. Aronson & Mills initiation study: severe initiation → rated boring group discussion more favorably. Key Study
Induced (Forced) Compliance — Being compelled to behave contrary to beliefs. Festinger & Carlsmith $1 vs. $20 study: those paid $1 (insufficient justification) rated boring task more favorably than those paid $20. Key Study
Insufficient Justification — Dissonance occurs when behavior is inconsistent with values AND the justification for the behavior is small. Small reward → more attitude change.
Conditions for Dissonance — (1) Freely chosen behavior, (2) Insufficient justification, (3) Negative consequences, (4) Foreseeable consequences.
Overjustification Effect — External rewards for intrinsically motivated behavior reduce internal motivation. Self-perception explanation: "I'm only doing this for the reward, so I don't really like it."
Self-Perception Theory (Bem) — Alternative to dissonance: people infer their own attitudes by observing their behavior, like outside observers do. More relevant when prior attitudes are vague/weak.
Reconciling Dissonance & Self-Perception — Both operate. Dissonance reduction for clear, important attitudes. Self-perception processes for vague, unimportant attitudes.
System Justification Theory (Jost) — People are motivated to defend existing sociopolitical systems as fair and legitimate, even if they're disadvantaged by them. Reduces ideological dissonance. Compensatory stereotypes example. Key Study
Cultural Differences in Dissonance — Japanese show dissonance in free-choice paradigm only when thinking about social others or when observed. Westerners show dissonance in standard conditions. Kitayama et al. poster study. Key Study
Terror Management Theory (TMT) — Confronting mortality increases defense of worldviews, ingroup loyalty, and striving for self-esteem. Mortality salience manipulations support this (Bush vs. Kerry study).

Persuasion

Chapter 8
Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) — Two routes to persuasion: Central (careful argument evaluation; high motivation/ability) and Peripheral (superficial cues; low motivation/ability). Petty & Cacioppo.
Central Route — Persuasion via careful, systematic analysis of message content and argument strength. Requires high motivation and ability. Produces more durable attitude change.
Peripheral Route — Persuasion via heuristics and superficial cues (source attractiveness, credibility, number of arguments) without careful thought. Common when personally irrelevant.
Three Elements of Persuasion (3 W's) — (1) Source ("who") — attractiveness, credibility, certainty; (2) Message ("what") — quality, vividness, fear; (3) Audience ("to whom") — need for cognition, mood, age.
Source Attractiveness — Attractive sources are more persuasive, especially via peripheral route. Triggers liking which transfers to attitude object.
Source Credibility — Expert and trustworthy sources are more persuasive. Credibility matters both centrally (as evidence) and peripherally (as heuristic).
Sleeper Effect — Over time, a noncredible source's message can become persuasive as source and message become dissociated. Also documented for credible sources with weak messages.
Need for Cognition — Individual difference in tendency to engage in and enjoy effortful thinking. High NFC individuals are more influenced by argument quality; low NFC by peripheral cues.
Subliminal Persuasion — Subliminal stimuli can prime concepts but NO evidence they induce behaviors people oppose or cause major attitude change. Judas Priest trial discussed.
Vividness Effect — Vivid, colorful, memorable information is more persuasive. Even when information is atypical (e.g., welfare queen anecdote still changes attitudes about welfare).
Fear Appeals — Effective when paired with concrete action steps. Film + pamphlet > film alone > pamphlet alone (Leventhal et al. smoking study).
Agenda Control — Media shapes what people think is important by controlling issue prominence. More stories about energy → more people cite it as a top problem.
Hostile Media Phenomenon — People perceive media coverage as biased against their side. Results from naive realism (believing one sees things objectively).
Attitude Inoculation (McGuire) — Exposing people to weak attacks on their beliefs, plus encouraging counterarguments, makes them resistant to future persuasion. Applied to fake news resistance. Key Study
Selective Attention & Exposure — People attend to and evaluate information in ways that confirm existing beliefs. Leads to "echo chambers." Evaluating our own health diagnoses (Ditto & Lopez).

Social Influence

Chapter 9
Social Influence Types — (1) Conformity: changing beliefs/behavior to align with others; (2) Compliance: responding favorably to explicit requests; (3) Obedience: submitting to authority demands.
Informational Social Influence — Conforming because others' behavior/opinions provide useful information about what's correct. Leads to internalization (private acceptance). Sherif's autokinetic illusion study. Key Study
Normative Social Influence — Conforming to avoid disapproval and negative social consequences. Leads to public compliance without private acceptance. Asch's line-matching study. Key Study
Sherif's Autokinetic Illusion Study — Participants in dark room converged their estimates of how far a light "moved." Group norms formed and persisted up to a year later. Demonstrates informational social influence. Key Study
Asch's Conformity Study — 8 participants (7 confederates) judged which line matched target. Confederates gave wrong answer. 75% conformed at least once; overall 33% conformity rate. Demonstrates normative social influence. Key Study
Factors Affecting Conformity — Group size (levels off at 3–4), unanimity (single ally dramatically reduces conformity), anonymity (reduces normative influence), expertise/status, cultural background (independent vs. interdependent), tight vs. loose cultures.
Tight vs. Loose Cultures (Gelfand) — Tight cultures: strong norms, little tolerance for deviance (India, Germany, China, Japan). Loose cultures: weaker norms, more deviance tolerated (Greece, Israel, Netherlands, US).
Milgram's Obedience Study — Participants as "teachers" administered increasing shocks to "learner" (confederate). 65% delivered maximum 450 volts in remote-feedback condition. Demonstrates destructive obedience to authority. Key Study
Why People Obeyed Milgram — Sense of obligation (agreed to participate), normative pressure (avoid experimenter disapproval), gradual escalation (stepwise), release of responsibility, physical distance from victim, perceived legitimacy of authority.
Milgram Moderating Factors — Proximity to learner ↓ obedience; physical distance from experimenter ↓ obedience; having an ally ↓ obedience; questioning authority's legitimacy ↓ obedience. Burger (2009) near-replication found similar rates.
Compliance Techniques — Foot-in-the-door (small request → large request), Door-in-the-face (large request → small request using reciprocal concessions), Norm of reciprocity, Descriptive vs. prescriptive norms.
Norm of Reciprocity — Universal social norm: if someone does something for you, you feel compelled to reciprocate. Cialdini's Boy Scout/chocolate bar experience illustrates door-in-the-face.
Pluralistic Ignorance — Acting contrary to true beliefs because you assume others don't share them, when in fact they do. Campus drinking example: students privately uncomfortable but assume everyone else is comfortable.
Reactance Theory — When freedom is threatened, motivation to restore it increases. If told not to do something, desire to do it increases. Explains some resistance to persuasion.
Minority Influence — Minorities can influence majorities through informational social influence, particularly if consistent, flexible, and persistent. Results in deeper, private attitude change vs. majority influence (surface compliance).

Relationships & Attraction

Chapter 10
Need to Belong — Baumeister & Leary: biologically based need for social belonging. Isolation increases mortality risk comparable to smoking. Harlow's monkey studies support.
Proximity Effect — Physical closeness leads to liking. MIT married-student housing study: people more likely to befriend those in same building/floor. Functional distance matters more than physical distance. Key Study
Mere Exposure Effect (Zajonc) — Repeated exposure to a stimulus increases liking of it. Works for faces, paintings, words (not auditory stimuli). Peaks around 35 exposures. Explains why proximity → attraction. Key Study
Similarity — People like others who are similar to themselves. More important for core values than surface traits. Consensual validation: similar others affirm our worldview.
Matching Hypothesis — People tend to pair with others of similar physical attractiveness (more supported). Complementarity hypothesis (opposites attract) has mixed evidence except for status exchange.
Halo Effect — Attractive people assumed to have many other positive qualities (happiness, intelligence, competence). Self-fulfilling prophecy: attractive people develop better social skills due to preferential treatment. Key Concept
Evolutionary Theory of Attraction — Mate preferences evolved to maximize reproductive fitness. Men prefer physical attractiveness and youth (signals fertility). Women prefer resources and status (signals ability to invest in offspring). Cross-cultural data from 37 cultures (Buss). BUT both genders rate kindness and intelligence most highly.
Socialization Critique (Eagly & Wood) — Gender differences in mate preferences may reflect socialization and structural gender inequality rather than evolution. In more gender-equal societies, differences are smaller.
Rewards Perspective on Relationships — We form and maintain relationships that provide rewards (especially making us feel good about ourselves).
Social Exchange Theory — People maximize rewards and minimize costs. Stay in relationships where rewards > costs. Key standards: comparison level (what you expect) and comparison level for alternatives (what you think you can get elsewhere).
Equity Theory — Relationships are satisfying when the ratio of rewards to costs is roughly equal for both partners. Fairness is the goal, not just profit maximization.
Investment Model (Rusbult) — Commitment depends on: (1) Satisfaction with the relationship, (2) Quality of alternatives (the worse, the more committed), (3) Size of investments (time, effort, shared memories). Key Model
Attachment Theory (Bowlby) — Early bonds with caregivers shape adult relationships. Three styles: (1) Secure (comfortable with closeness), (2) Avoidant (uncomfortable with intimacy, distrusts), (3) Anxious-Ambivalent (fears abandonment, wants merger).
Varieties of Love — Companionate love (affection in long-term bonds), Compassionate love (caring about others' well-being), Romantic/passionate love (intense desire for another). Also Sternberg's components: intimacy, passion, commitment.
Predictors of Relationship Dissatisfaction — Neuroticism, low self-esteem, rejection sensitivity, young age at marriage, lower SES, poor communication. Gottman's "Four Horsemen": criticism, stonewalling, defensiveness, contempt (most toxic).
Strengthening Relationships — Capitalizing on good news (active constructive responses), playfulness and humor, idealizing partner (seeing virtues in faults), perceived partner responsiveness.
Communal vs. Exchange Relationships — Communal: long-term, based on need, family-like (Clark & Mills). Exchange: short-term, equity- and reciprocity-based (transactions).
